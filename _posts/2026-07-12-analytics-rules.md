Analytics rules are what actually turn raw log ingestion in Sentinel into alerts and incidents, and the gap between a rule that looks reasonable on paper and one that survives contact with a real, noisy production environment is usually where most of a detection engineer's actual time goes.

## The rule types and what they're for

Sentinel supports a handful of distinct analytics rule types, and picking the right one matters more than the KQL logic itself. Scheduled rules are the workhorse, running a KQL query on an interval you define, evaluating results against a threshold, and generating an alert when that threshold is met; this covers the vast majority of custom detection logic teams write. NRT, near-real-time rules, run every minute rather than on a longer schedule and are reserved for a limited set of high-priority detections where the extra evaluation frequency justifies the added cost. Microsoft Security rules automatically create Sentinel incidents from alerts already generated by connected Microsoft security products like Defender for Endpoint or Defender for Identity, without you writing any KQL at all. Fusion rules apply Microsoft's own multi-stage correlation logic across different data sources to catch attack patterns that span several low-severity signals which wouldn't individually trigger anything on their own.

## What makes a rule survive production

A rule that fires constantly gets muted or ignored within weeks, which defeats the entire point of writing it. The practical levers that separate a rule people trust from one that gets silently disabled are entity mapping, alert grouping, and suppression. Entity mapping ties specific fields in your query results, like an account name or an IP address, to Sentinel's recognized entity types, and that mapping is what enables the automatic incident correlation across multiple rules and multiple alerts firing on the same underlying entity, rather than each alert existing as an island. Alert grouping settings control whether multiple triggering events over time get bundled into one incident or spawn a new incident every single time, and getting this wrong is one of the most common causes of a queue that looks far busier than the actual underlying activity justifies.

## Tuning is the actual job

Writing the initial query is the easy part. The real work is watching a new rule's fidelity over its first couple of weeks live, checking the false positive rate against actual environment behavior, and iterating the query logic, whether that means adding an exclusion for a known noisy service account, tightening a threshold, or adding a join against a watchlist of expected admin activity. Sentinel's incident feedback options let analysts mark closed incidents as true or false positive directly, and that feedback loop, if anyone's actually reviewing it in aggregate rather than just closing tickets, is the fastest way to spot which rules need tuning without waiting for a formal quarterly review.

## Where teams get it wrong

Importing Microsoft's built-in rule templates wholesale without adjusting thresholds for the specific environment is the most common mistake, since a threshold tuned for a generic reference environment rarely matches the actual volume and pattern of activity in any specific tenant. The second common mistake is writing detections that only cover the exact technique observed once, in a specific past incident, rather than the broader technique family, which produces a rule that will reliably miss the same attack next time it shows up with a slightly different command line or process name.

A good analytics rule isn't the one with the cleverest KQL, it's the one an analyst still trusts enough to act on immediately six months after it was written, and that trust is earned through tuning, not through the initial deployment.
